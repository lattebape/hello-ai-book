# Preface · You've Mastered It — Now What?

> FIG: 配图0-1-同行的路.svg

In Book One, you figured out what it is. In Book Two, you got good at using it. By rights, the story could end there.

But the more fluent you get, the more a new kind of question keeps surfacing —

In the weekly meeting you think: it can do most of these tasks — what does my job look like in five years? Helping with homework you think: it produces answers instantly — should the kids still memorize anything? On the phone with your parents you think: even I was nearly fooled by a cloned voice — what about them? Scrolling you think: this video looks so real — is it? Watching the news you think: this endless shouting about "AI regulation" — what is it actually about?

Notice something? Not one of these is a *how do I use it* question. Every one is a **how do we live** question.

That is why Book Three exists. The first two books starred "me and AI." This one stars **us and AI**: your work, your children, your parents, the world you see through your screen, and this changing era we all share.

First, what this book will **not** do: no prophecy (Book One's roller coaster taught us how often the prophets miss), no anxiety-mongering (anxiety solves nothing), and no taking sides for you (on the loudest controversies, you'll get both sides' reasons and draw your own conclusion).

It will do exactly one thing: lay the changes out on the table, so you can **see them clearly, think them through, and stand on solid ground.**

Yoyo and Bubble are still with you. This is the final stretch of the trilogy — let's go.

# Chapter 1 · What Happens to Work

> FIG: 配图1-1-新工位.svg

## First, Fix the Question

"Will AI take my job?" — the most-asked question of the era, and it's asked in the wrong unit.

The key to seeing clearly is to move your eyes from *jobs* to *tasks*: **no job is a solid slab; every job is a bundle of tasks.** An accountant's job bundles data entry, reconciliation, reports, tax planning, and arguing with the sales team. A teacher's job bundles lesson prep, teaching, grading, heart-to-hearts, and parent meetings.

AI doesn't move the parcel called "your job" — it takes individual items out of the parcel. So the right question becomes: **in my bundle, which tasks will it take, and which will stay — and grow more valuable?**

## Which Tasks Go First

You can derive it from Book One's principles. What it does best is **formatted work with mountains of precedent to learn from and results that are easy to check** — first drafts, sorting and filing, translation, routine Q&A, template reports. Exactly the work you handed it in Book Two.

Conversely, three kinds of tasks it cannot take — matching, one for one, the capability borders from Book One:

**Tasks needing real-world experience** — calming a sobbing customer, improvising on a construction site. It has read everything and lived nothing (Moravec's paradox).

**Tasks needing accountable decisions** — signing, pricing, making the call. It wants nothing and can bear no consequences; responsibility can't be outsourced.

**Tasks needing human trust** — negotiating, leading, keeping a long-time client. Trust only grows between people.

> FIG: b3-diag1-1-EN.svg

## History's Script: Few Vanish, Most Get Rewritten

This isn't humanity's first time. When ATMs arrived, everyone said bank tellers were finished — tellers didn't vanish; their work shifted from counting cash to advising customers. When spreadsheets arrived, accountants didn't vanish; they moved up from bookkeeping to analysis and planning.

The usual script of a technology revolution isn't *jobs evaporate* but *jobs get rewritten*: machines take the standardizable part, and people get pushed toward judgment, creativity, and the human touch. The script does have genuinely eliminated roles — usually the ones whose entire bundle was formatted work. Honestly: the change is real and so is the pain; its shape is reshuffling, not the "wipeout" of the headlines.

## Three Things to Do Now

**One: give your task list a checkup.** Write down the five most time-consuming things in your work and run Book Two's traffic light over them. Green — actively learn to hand them to AI; early movers become the most efficient person on the team. Red — dig deeper on purpose; that's your moat.

**Two: become the one on your team who's good with AI.** Same job title, widening gap in output. This window is the bonus round: the tools are public, the skilled users are still few.

**Three: reinvest the saved time in what machines can't take.** One more client visit, one more junior mentored, one more judgment sharpened. Once AI takes the formatted labor, **your value equals the sum of your judgment and your human touch** — invest there and you can't lose.

## Chapter Takeaways

1. Don't ask "will my job disappear" — ask "which tasks in my bundle get taken": the formatted ones go; experience, accountability, and trust stay.
2. History's script is rewrite over erase: machines take the standardizable, people move toward judgment, creativity, warmth — real change, reshuffle-shaped.
3. Three moves: run the task checkup and hand over the greens, become your team's AI person, reinvest saved time where machines can't follow.

## Try It Yourself: The Task Checkup

Write down the five most time-consuming things in your job. For each, mark: Is there a mountain of precedent for this? Is the result easy to verify? Two yeses — green: let AI take half of it this week. Any no — red: write one sentence on how you'll go deeper.

That sheet is your transition map for the next three years.

# Chapter 2 · An AI Lesson for the Kids

> FIG: 配图2-1-一起写作业.svg

## The Question That Torments Parents

It answers homework on sight and writes essays on command — so should kids still memorize poems and drill arithmetic? Clamp down and you fear they'll fall behind; let go and you fear they'll go soft. Probably the most agonizing parenting question of the AI era.

This chapter won't serve a standard answer, but it can take the question apart. First, half the reassurance up front: **calculators didn't kill math class, and AI won't kill learning.** After calculators, we stopped drilling square roots by hand — and needed even more to know *when to divide and when to multiply*. Likewise, AI has taken over the labor of *writing it out*, which only raises the value of *thinking it through*.

## The Key Distinction: Whose Brain Made the Turn

Book Two Chapter 6 drew a line; for children it must be drawn even sharper, because their self-control is still under construction:

**AI as tutor — encourage it.** "I don't get this problem; walk me through the approach, don't give the answer." "Quiz me on this lesson's vocabulary." The turn in the brain is the child's own; AI is just the tireless sparring partner.

**AI as ghostwriter — forbid it.** Copying answers, outsourcing essays. The machine made the turn; what gets handed in is homework, what doesn't get handed in is ability. And kids are sharp: once they discover the turn can be skipped, willingness to make it fades fast.

The touchstone is the same as ever: **close the device — can the child explain the problem to you?**

## Three House Rules for the Fridge Door

Principles don't execute; rules do. Three that work as written:

**Rule one: think first, then ask AI.** Homework starts by hand; stuck problems get marked, *then* asked about — for approach, not answers. The order can't flip: reading the answer first and then "understanding it" is learning's number-one illusion.

**Rule two: whatever AI said, you can retell.** After the child asks AI, have them explain it to you. If it comes out, it's learned; if not, back it goes. This rule quietly solves "but I can't tutor this subject" — you don't need to know the problem, you only need to be an audience.

**Rule three: use it together, not in secret.** Younger kids use AI in the living room, within grown-up sight. Not surveillance — company. And you get to see what they ask and what it answers.

> FIG: b3-diag2-1-EN.svg

## Three Abilities That Matter More Than Solving Problems

Zoom out one level. If machines keep getting better at *writing it out* and *computing it*, what children should stockpile is what machines can't:

**Asking good questions.** The cheaper answers get, the dearer questions become. One more "why do you think that?" at dinner beats ten more drill sheets.

**Telling real from fake.** This generation is born into a hard-to-tell information world (two chapters from now). "Is this true? How would we check?" should become as instinctive as looking both ways before crossing.

**Real-world experience.** Climbing trees, cooking, quarreling and making up — machines have no childhood, and that is precisely the most irreplaceable human asset. The more omnipotent the screen, the more precious the mud.

And notice: which of these three is new? None. **The AI era invented no new goals for education — it just highlighted the most expensive lines of the old ones.**

## Chapter Takeaways

1. AI won't kill learning any more than calculators killed math — "writing it out" is devalued, "thinking it through" is revalued.
2. Tutor yes, ghostwriter no; the touchstone is retelling. Three house rules: think first, retell after, use it together.
3. Stockpile what machines can't: good questions, truth-detection, real-world experience — the old goals' most expensive lines, now highlighted.

## Try It Yourself: A Role Swap

This week, pick a question your child asked AI, and have the child teach it to you — you play the student who loves asking "why."

Fluent and beaming — relax. Halting and stuck — no scolding; go back to AI together for one more round, this time with the child knowing "I'll have to teach this to Mom or Dad after." Watch the quality of their questions transform on the spot.

# Chapter 3 · Helping Your Parents Across the Digital Divide

> FIG: 配图3-1-教爸妈.svg

## This Time, We're the Teachers

Once upon a time they taught us chopsticks, shoelaces, and how to cross the street. In the digital world the roles have quietly reversed: we taught them QR codes and video calls — and now, AI.

This isn't about keeping up with fashion. For older people, AI may be *more* useful than it is for us: **ask anything without hesitation** (no fear of bothering the kids), **company for the quiet hours** (opera, old songs, stories from their youth — it keeps up with all of it), and **one more gate against scams** (the most important part; second half of this chapter).

## The First Barrier Isn't Intelligence — It's Psychology

Anyone who has taught parents to use a phone knows the slowdowns usually aren't cognitive. They're three worries: **afraid of breaking it, afraid of being a bother, afraid of being laughed at.**

Conveniently — AI is the antidote to exactly those three: it can't be broken (say it wrong, just say it again); it can't be bothered (the hundredth ask gets the same patience as the first); and it never, ever laughs at anyone.

Read that paragraph to your parents word for word. For many elders, that reassurance *is* the missing first step.

## Voice Is the Elder Expressway

Do not let typing stand in their way. Teach one motion and one only: **hold the microphone button and talk.**

"If you can send a voice message, you can use AI" — for older users this sentence is revolutionary. Pinyin, handwriting, tiny keyboards: all bypassed. Teach voice in lesson one and the success rate doubles.

## One Real Need, Not Ten Features

The classic mistake: demonstrating eighteen features in one sitting, dazzling them into remembering none. The right move is **teach exactly one scenario they genuinely need.** Once it sticks, they'll come asking for the second one themselves.

Which one? Read the person: the health-conscious get "ask it anything" — "can I eat pickles with high blood pressure?" (and state the red-light rule plainly: **for medicine and treatment, the doctor decides; AI is reference only**). The grandkid-obsessed get "riddles for the grandson." The opera lover gets a chat companion. The weak-eyed get photo questions: "what is this, what does this label say?"

## The Anti-Scam Lesson Matters Most

Book Two Chapter 8's "hang up — call back — code word" must be taught to your parents intact, plus one simplified rule of thumb made for them:

**Any request for money: hang up first, then call family.**

No matter if the caller is a "grandson" or "the police," no matter how real the voice or how urgent the story — hang up, then dial the family's own numbers. Set the code word this week (one small fact only the family knows), and add one more line: **don't tap unfamiliar links; when unsure, ask family first.**

Teach this one lesson well and the chapter has already paid for itself.

## Patience Is the Best Teaching Aid

Last, a word for us, the grown children: they may ask about the same step five times. Recall how many times we asked "why" — easily fifty.

Two tricks: write the steps on a **large-print card** tucked into the phone case (step one press here, step two say this); and after each lesson, **watch them do it themselves once** — fingers remember better than heads.

> FIG: b3-diag3-1-EN.svg

## Chapter Takeaways

1. The elder barrier is psychological, not intellectual: AI can't be broken, bothered, or made to laugh at them — hand them that reassurance first.
2. Lesson one is voice (hold and talk); teach one genuinely needed scenario at a time; health questions welcome, but medicine belongs to doctors.
3. The anti-scam kit is non-negotiable: money requests mean hang up and call family, set the code word, don't tap unknown links — the most valuable lesson in the chapter.

## Try It Yourself: This Week's Filial Homework

Three steps, under an hour: install an AI assistant on your parents' phone; teach them to voice-ask one question they care about (watch them succeed with their own hands); agree on the family code word together.

Do those three and you've done more than teach — you've delivered the first two books' value, doubled, to the people who need it most.

# Chapter 4 · The Age of Hard to Tell

> FIG: 配图4-1-真假之间.svg

## A Video in the Family Group Chat

Picture this (it happens somewhere every day): in the family group chat, an elder forwards a video — a famous doctor, on camera, solemnly endorsing a miracle drug. Voice, lip movements, mannerisms: all check out.

One problem: the doctor never said any of it. The video is AI-made.

Book One Chapter 5 showed you AI painting pictures and left a warning: "seeing is no longer believing." In this book the warning gets an upgrade: **it's not just pictures — voices, photos, and video can all be manufactured, and they keep getting better.** The technology has a name — deepfakes — and it has moved from the lab into the family group chat.

## Why Fakes Suddenly Multiplied

You already know the principle: generative AI has learned the *look* of the world — how faces move, how voices waver, how light falls — so it can produce convincing new samples.

What changed the game is cost: **a fake video used to take a professional team; now it takes minutes and pocket change.** When faking gets cheap, fakes get plentiful; when fakes get plentiful, your odds of meeting one get real. This isn't a "someday, be careful" forecast — it's a "you need this now" fact.

## The Three-Question Check

The good news: beating it takes no technology, just three questions. When something suspicious crosses your screen, run through:

**Question one: who's the source?** An established outlet or official account — or a no-name account registered yesterday with gibberish in the handle? Stories can lie; track records mostly can't.

**Question two: who else is saying so?** Genuinely big news is never carried by one outlet alone. Search it — if the whole internet has exactly one source, hold it in doubt and set it aside.

**Question three: does it hit your feelings a little too perfectly?** The sharpest question of the three. Fake news survives by **making you feel** — the more furious, frightened, or fired-up something makes you, the slower you should go. Real news can survive your calm; fake news can't.

> FIG: b3-diag4-1-EN.svg

## Two Habits Worth a Dozen Tricks

**Habit one: wait ten minutes before forwarding.** Misinformation runs on speed; deny it speed and you've halved it. Still worth forwarding after ten minutes? Forward away.

**Habit two: escalate verification for things that matter.** Anything touching money, health, or family decisions gets Book Two Chapter 8's tiered check — confirm through official channels. And note: the AI-cloned "relative in trouble" call belongs to this chapter too; the three-step drill (hang up — call back — code word) is still on duty.

Keep one plain piece of arithmetic in mind: **if you don't forward it, the rumor's chain breaks at you.** Everyone is one link; this is the most concrete contribution an ordinary person can make.

## Don't Overcorrect into Believing Nothing

One last pull on the steering wheel: leaping from "believe everything" to "believe nothing" lands you in a different pit — one the fakers actively enjoy: when people trust nothing, truth-tellers lose their power too.

Information literacy is not suspicion of everything; it is **a gate on your trust**: reliable source, multiple confirmations — believe and move on; fails the three questions — set aside. Like the crosswalk rule you learned as a child: check the light. It never meant "never cross the street."

## Chapter Takeaways

1. Voices, photos, and video can all be faked now, cheaply — "seeing is believing" has officially retired; this is present tense, not prophecy.
2. The three-question check: who's the source? who else says so? does it hit the feelings too perfectly? Fail one — don't forward.
3. Two habits: ten-minute delay before forwarding, escalate checks for what matters; your not-forwarding breaks the chain. And don't overcorrect into total distrust — literacy is a gate on trust, not the demolition of it.

## Try It Yourself: The Check's First Case

Scroll your recent group chats for one message you're unsure about, run the three questions, and note what you find.

If it turns out fake, tell the person who forwarded it — gently: "I looked it up; several official channels have debunked this one, here's the link." The secret to correcting without bruising: address the claim not the person, carry evidence not blame.

# Chapter 5 · Earning a Living in the AI Era

> FIG: 配图5-1-技能行囊.svg

## Chapter 1's Question Comes Home

Chapter 1 mapped the big picture: jobs are being reshuffled. This chapter zooms in on the more personal question: **so what should I learn, and what do I do?**

Set the compass first: facing the reshuffle, there are exactly two directions — **go deeper into what machines can't take**, and **learn to work with the machines**. The first sets your ceiling; the second sets your speed. The good news: you're already moving on both — the second is precisely the craft Book Two spent nine chapters on.

## Three Assets That Hold Their Value

Which abilities appreciate rather than depreciate in the AI era? The inventory comes to three, each echoing earlier chapters:

**Asset one: judgment.** Anyone can look things up; the machine can draft ten plans — **picking the right one and answering for it** is human work. Taste, trade-offs, the final call: this ability compounds with use, and the stronger machines get, the scarcer it becomes (Chapter 1: accountable tasks stay with people).

**Asset two: people skills.** Soothing, persuading, coordinating, mentoring, being the one clients trust with the job. Trust only grows between people — that sentence appeared in Chapter 1, and it doubles as career advice.

**Asset three: the craft of asking.** The core of "working with machines." On the same AI, the entire gap in output lives in the asker (you watched the before-and-after yourself, Book Two Chapter 2). Translating a fuzzy situation into a clear question — for the machine, for the client, for yourself — is graduating from "soft skill" to hard currency.

> FIG: b3-diag5-1-EN.svg

## Learning Something New Has Never Been Cheaper

Any pivot runs through learning, and here's the underrated fact: **the cost of learning anything new is at its lowest point in human history** — because Book Two Chapter 6's tutor lives in your pocket.

A four-step path you can actually run:

**Step one, pick the direction**: from your task-checkup sheet (Chapter 1's exercise) — the red items you chose to deepen, or the new work the "AI-fluent" people in your field are doing.

**Step two, AI-tutor the basics**: the three magic lines — "explain it to me," "quiz me," "practice with me" — build the scaffolding in two weeks.

**Step three, practice on real work**: apply it immediately to the job in front of you, even if it starts with helping a colleague on one small piece. Skills only grow muscle in real work.

**Step four, teach one person**: the Feynman sign-off. If you can teach it, it's officially in your bag.

## A Pivot Isn't a Cliff Jump — It's Bricklaying

"Career pivot" conjures resignations and burned boats. For most people the right posture is far plainer: **stay in your current job, hand one task to AI each week, learn one new thing with AI each month.**

A brick a week, a layer a month. Look back in two years and you're standing somewhere new — with no stunt work involved.

## One Honest Sentence

This chapter won't end with "such-and-such profession is safe," because nobody can underwrite a specific job. What can be underwritten are two probabilities: **people who can use AI will be calmer than those who can't, and people who keep learning will be calmer than those who stop.**

The boat is already in the waves. Rather than asking which cabin rocks least, learn to swim. This trilogy is the swimming lesson.

## Chapter Takeaways

1. Two directions at once: deeper into what machines can't take (ceiling), fluent at working with machines (speed) — you trained the second in Book Two.
2. Three appreciating assets: judgment (choose and answer for it), people skills (the trust business), the craft of asking (fuzzy situations into clear questions) — the stronger the machines, the scarcer these get.
3. Learning is historically cheap: pick → AI-tutor → real work → teach to confirm. Pivoting is bricklaying, not cliff-jumping — one task a week, one skill a month.

## Try It Yourself: First Lesson Tonight

Write down one direction you want to learn (small is fine: pivot tables, video editing, the new industry rules), and take the first lesson tonight with the three magic lines. Twenty minutes.

Big pivots should start exactly this small — because this small can be repeated tomorrow night.

# Chapter 6 · What Is Everyone Arguing About?

> FIG: 配图6-1-圆桌.svg

## Why Listen to Other People's Arguments

Open the news: artists signing protest letters, giants suing each other; one country passes an AI act, another announces deregulation. Noise everywhere — does any of it touch daily life?

It does, directly — **the outcomes of these arguments become the fine print of your life**: whether the photos you post can be used for training, whether your child's classroom uses AI, whether your industry gets a safety net when the wave hits. Reading these arguments is reading where the era is headed.

House rules, stated up front: four arguments, and **each one gets both sides' reasons laid on the table.** No refereeing, no nudging — the verdict is yours.

## Argument One: Copyright — Should the Machine Ask Before It Learns From Me?

**Creators say**: AI grew up reading our words and studying our paintings — no permission asked, no fee paid; then it imitates our style and competes for our work. Where's the justice? License it and share the money.

**AI companies and some scholars say**: humans learn to paint by copying the masters; learning from public works is the normal flow of knowledge. Require a negotiated license for every scrap of training data and the technology can't exist — then nobody gets its benefits.

Status: courts are deciding case by case; new rules are still hardening. Both sides carry real grievances and real reasons — which is exactly why it's hard.

## Argument Two: Privacy — Where Does Our Data Go?

**The worried say**: AI is raised on data — are our chats, photos, and movements in the feed bin? Users deserve, at minimum, the right to know, to refuse, and to delete.

**The industry says**: data is the raw material of improvement; mainstream companies anonymize and scrub it. Lock the data away and the products stop getting better.

On this one you needn't wait for the verdict — Book Two Chapter 8's do-not-feed list is the personal-side defense: let the institutions argue slowly; control at the source starts today.

## Argument Three: Jobs — Who Carries the Pain?

**The concerned say**: "reshuffle" is a macro word; on one person it lands as a lost job. Society should have retraining and a floor ready — don't bill individuals for a systemic shift.

**The optimistic say**: from steam to the internet, every technology revolution eventually created more jobs than it destroyed; overprotecting old jobs slows the birth of new ones.

Notably, there's more consensus here than the shouting suggests: almost nobody opposes "helping the displaced transition." The real split is over how fast the change comes — and who pays.

## Argument Four: Regulation — Brakes First, or Speed First?

**The strict side says**: greater capability, greater risk — fake information, new scams, runaway automated decisions. Cars get brakes before they get roads; don't write the rules after the crash.

**The light-touch side says**: rules set too early and too rigid chase innovation elsewhere; and strict rules tend to bind the companies that follow them while missing the people who don't.

Status: countries are testing different recipes — legislate first, watch first, regulate by sector. No standard answer exists; the whole world is crossing this river by feeling the stones.

> FIG: b3-diag6-1-EN.svg

## Where Ordinary People Come In

Having heard all four, you might ask: do I even get a say?

You do, and in everyday forms: **know both sides before you take one** (even in a group chat — every reasoned voice dilutes a shouting one); **vote with your choices** (prefer products that respect privacy; support creators who honestly label AI's role); **speak when comment periods open** (many rules invite public input before adoption — that's an ordinary person's voice, priced in and counted).

Public debate never suffers from too many informed participants. The more people who understand both sides, the better the rules that come out — which is this chapter's reason for existing.

## Chapter Takeaways

1. Four arguments will become your life's fine print: copyright (learning freedom vs. creators' rights), privacy (better products vs. informed control), jobs (transition support vs. speed of progress), regulation (rules first vs. run first).
2. Every side holds real reasons — that's precisely the difficulty. Distrust any answer that fits in one sentence.
3. Three everyday ways in: know both sides before taking one, vote with your choices, speak in comment periods. Personal defenses (the do-not-feed list) need no permission to start.

## Try It Yourself: A Family Debate

Pick the argument that touches you most and stage a ten-minute debate with family or friends: each takes a side and argues it in earnest. Then **swap sides** and go again.

A view that survives the swap is an opinion; one that can't make the crossing was just a mood. This exercise pays better than any op-ed you'll read this year.

# Chapter 7 · A Glance Down the Road

> FIG: 配图7-1-望远镜.svg

## Looking Far Is Fine; Fortune-Telling Is Not

One glance ahead before the trilogy's last stop.

Ground rules first. Book One's roller coaster taught us that prophets in this field get slapped by reality on schedule — the "never possible" crowd was wrong, and so was the "next year for sure" crowd. So this chapter predicts no five-years-from-now world. It covers **three directions already on the road** — not "will happen" but "is happening" — and issues each one the usual pair of glasses: *can already / can't yet*.

## Direction One: Agents — from Answering to Doing

The AI you used in the first two books runs on "you ask, it answers." The step taking shape is the **agent**: you state a goal, and it breaks down the steps, gathers information, operates tools, and gets the thing done — "help me plan a trip" becomes "build the itinerary, compare the prices, list what needs my confirmation."

**Can already**: chain multi-step lookups, organization, and early execution — a genuinely decent intern.

**Can't yet**: be left alone. It errs without noticing (the old hallucination ailment in a more capable body), and the more steps, the harder errors snowball. So the traffic light gains a clause: **the more doing-power you grant it, the more often a human checks** — above all where money and irreversible actions live.

## Direction Two: Robots — AI Grows Hands and Feet

Chat AI is a brain in a jar; the next step is a body for the brain. Warehouse sorting arms, hotel delivery carts, ever-smarter robot vacuums — the advance party is here.

**Can already**: repetitive work in structured environments — tidy setting, clear task, fast and steady.

**Can't yet**: fine work in the messy real world the way people do it. Clearing a chaotic dinner table, turning and bathing an elderly patient — Book One's Moravec's paradox is still on duty: **easiest for humans, hardest for machines.** The all-purpose household robot butler needs more time.

## Direction Three: Science Assistants — Accelerating Discovery

The quietest direction, possibly the most profound: AI is doing chores for scientists. Predicting protein structures, screening candidate materials and drugs, helping doctors read scans — compressing years of sifting into weeks.

**Can already**: search vast spaces of possibility with brutal efficiency — the scientist's super-magnifier and sieve.

**Can't yet**: pose the scientist's good questions, or answer to you for your health. **It is an accelerator, not a discoverer** — the steering wheel stays in human hands. You know this sentence pattern well by now.

> FIG: b3-diag7-1-EN.svg

## Three Postures for Facing the Future

Directions age; postures don't. Whatever the future specifically looks like, these three won't be wrong:

**Stay curious about new capabilities.** New tool ships — dare to try it. With Book Two's craft as your base, every new AI tool is an old acquaintance in new clothes.

**Stay skeptical of new promises.** The hotter the hype, the brighter your traffic light should run. "This changes everything" arrives annually; you drilled the verification in Chapter 4.

**Keep investing in what doesn't change.** Judgment, people skills, the craft of asking, real-world experience, the people beside you — the higher the waves, the more the ballast is worth.

## Chapter Takeaways

1. Looking far, not fortune-telling: three directions already on the road — agents (from answering to doing), robots (AI grows a body), science assistants (accelerating discovery).
2. Each has its can't-yet: agents err without noticing (more power → more checking), robots can't handle the messy real world, science assistants accelerate but don't discover.
3. Three postures beat all forecasts: curious about capabilities, skeptical of promises, invested in what doesn't change.

## Try It Yourself: A Time Capsule for Yourself

Starting today, jot down three "AI is about to change everything" claims you hear in the news, with dates.

In six months, open the capsule and grade them: which came true? which vanished quietly? The game trains not prediction but **the calibration of your own judgment.** Two rounds in, you'll read tech news with different eyes.

# Chapter 8 · Epilogue: The Human Seat

> FIG: 配图8-1-回望来路.svg

## Three Doors, All Behind Us Now

Remember? Every book ended with a door. Look back: all three now stand behind you.

**Inside the first door**, we took the machine apart and saw it plainly: a machine that learns patterns from data, playing a word-chain game — astonishing and absurd by turns, the two faces of one coin.

**Inside the second door**, we worked it into a comfortable tool: blocks to start, follow-ups to finish, a traffic light for the division of labor, a card box for the growing feel of it. From a word in the news to a helper in your pocket.

**Inside the third door**, we lifted our eyes to the world it is entering: work mid-reshuffle, the child at the desk, the parents on the phone, the half-true screen, the bag worth packing, the round table that won't stop arguing, and three roads in a telescope.

Three books, one job the whole way through: **walking with you as "strange" turned into "familiar."** Because what people truly fear was never the machine. It was the strangeness.

## So Where Is the Human Seat?

The whole trilogy has been answering a single question. Now it can be said in full:

The machine can write — **the human answers for telling the truth.** The machine has answers — **the human supplies the questions.** The machine computes fast — **the human makes the call, and carries it.** The machine has read everything — **the human has lived: loved, ached, grown up somewhere.** It holds all the world's text; you hold the one childhood the world has no copy of.

Gathered into one line: **AI's seat is the tool's seat; the human seat is behind the wheel.**

Technology will keep changing — faster and stranger than these three books could record. The seating chart should not. And the way to hold the chart in place is neither to resist the machine nor to worship it — it is exactly what these three books practiced: **understand it, use it well, and then, with it along, live your own life well.**

## Three Things for the Road

Time to say goodbye. If the bag holds only three things, take these:

**Curiosity without fear** — Book One's gift. Whoever has seen the gears turn cannot be spooked by a headline.

**Hands-on, not hands-off** — Book Two's gift. A tool's worth lives in use; one thing done beats a hundred things understood.

**Clear-eyed, not anxious** — Book Three's gift. See the change and the unchanging; hear both sides and still make up your own mind.

## Goodbye

Yoyo and Bubble's story ends here.

The last exercise isn't written in the book — it's written outside it: **tell someone who hasn't read these pages what you learned here.** Your parents, your child, your friend. Any book of the three, any chapter.

Because what this trilogy wanted was never to be finished — it was for "understand AI, use AI well, live well with AI" to travel onward, through you, to one more person.

The story continues at your place.

Goodbye, friend. May you and this new era get along beautifully.
